A deeper view of the present environment shows that data fragmentation is not limited to
one office transaction. It propagates across the entire student lifecycle, from enrollment
intake and payment posting to section assignment, attendance recording, and report
generation. According to Zulueta et al. (2021), MIS environments in educational
institutions fail when workflow standardization is weak, even when digital tools are
available. This finding mirrors Marriott School's operational reality where records may
exist in digital format but still require repeated reconciliation before they can be trusted for
release, compliance, or planning.

In enrollment periods, the registrar must process a large volume of learner entries under
time pressure while preserving LIS-oriented consistency. When intake records are encoded
in disconnected trackers, every downstream update introduces a new opportunity for
mismatch. According to Porlas et al. (2023), inconsistencies between LIS-facing structures
and school-level MIS records create recurring validation burden across offices. For
Marriott School, this means that what appears as a small intake encoding delay can later
affect payment confirmation, class-list finalization, and administrative reporting
confidence.

The record-continuity requirement extends beyond initial enrollment. Permanent records,
promotion decisions, and student-departure handling all depend on the same validated
learner identity baseline. If this baseline is fragmented, later academic and compliance
outputs become harder to verify and slower to release. According to Kanona (2022),
structured database-backed systems provide stronger retrieval reliability and control
integrity than manual, file-fragmented record handling. This supports MarriottConnect's
design principle that learner data should be encoded with continuity in mind rather than re-
encoded at every stage of institutional processing.

directly affects trust, cash-flow transparency, and release-dependent school transactions.
