Appendix interviews confirm that this coordination remains difficult under fragmented
tooling. The Academic Head explained that departments still maintain separate Excel files
and frequently reconcile numbers manually before reports can be finalized. This condition
creates a recurring pattern where staff attention shifts away from planning and student
support toward repeated checking, correction, and consolidation tasks that should already
be systematized.

The registrar workflow shows this problem in concrete terms. Mrs. Jocelyn Cleofe
described enrollment and record updates as one-by-one processing with repeated cross-
checking across spreadsheets and forms, especially during high-volume periods.
According to Grepon et al. (2021), integrated school systems reduce delays and improve
record reliability when admissions and records processing operate on one shared data
structure. Kanona (2022) further emphasizes that centralized database-backed
management improves retrieval speed and record integrity compared with manual, file-
fragmented handling.

Finance operations demonstrate the same fragmentation in monetary form. Mrs. Corrine
Avellanosa noted that payment updates often require manual cross-referencing before
balances are confidently released, particularly when multiple transactions occur close
together. According to Wulandari and Pinandito (2021), tuition-related decision support
becomes more consistent when financial status logic is structured and system-driven
rather than manually interpreted. Chai and Mostafa (2021) similarly report that integrated
financial and student records reduce duplication and improve cross-user visibility.

Academic coordination is likewise affected. The registrar and teacher interviews indicate
that schedule preparation, class-list alignment, and grade validation involve repetitive
verification cycles before release. According to Muller et al. (2025), timetable quality and
conflict reduction depend on explicit constraint handling rather than manual plotting.
Zulueta et al. (2021) also show that process standardization and workflow discipline are
critical in educational MIS environments, reinforcing the need for structured academic
operations across sections, subjects, and grade submissions.
