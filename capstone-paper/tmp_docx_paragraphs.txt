0095: Introduction
0096: Educational institutions increasingly rely on integrated information systems to maintain operational efficiency, data accuracy, and timely institutional decision-making. The effectiveness of how services are provided every day in schools where the operations are interconnected from an institutional administrative perspective, academic perspective and financial perspective is determined by the accuracy, reliability and availability of information. When there is only one time encoding of data through to its correct usage throughout the organization, the efficiency of workflow, reliability of reporting and stakeholders are able to receive their updates in a more timely manner. However, if school operations are still fragmented through various types of spreadsheets, paper logs and separate departmental records, then the school is at risk of having duplicate data, conflicting records and prolonged validation cycles. 
0097: The value of management information systems has long been associated with reducing procedural burden and improving reporting quality in multi-office environments. In contrast, fragmented systems often make institutions data-heavy but insight-poor, because data exists in large volume yet remains difficult to consolidate into usable, decision-ready outputs.
0098: In the Philippine education context, the need for integrated school operations is even more pronounced because compliance-driven reporting and routine internal processing must be performed simultaneously. For Marriott School, this dual demand exposes the limits of disconnected tools and manual reconciliation practices. In response, this study proposes MarriottConnect as an integrated student information system with decision support analytics, designed to centralize operational records, reduce repetitive encoding, and provide leadership with structured evidence for planning and continuous improvement.




0099: Project Context
0100: Marriott School operates in a DepEd-governed environment where learner identity, enrollment reporting, and school-level operational records must remain synchronized through LIS-oriented procedures. According to Porlas et al. (2023), schools encounter higher validation burden and cross-office reporting inconsistencies when local MIS workflows are not tightly aligned with LIS structures. For Marriott School, this requirement is not merely technical compliance; it is an operational discipline that directly affects how registrar, finance, and academic offices coordinate daily decisions.
0101: Appendix interviews confirm that this coordination remains difficult under fragmented tooling. The Academic Head explained that departments still maintain separate Excel files and frequently reconcile numbers manually before reports can be finalized. This condition creates a recurring pattern where staff attention shifts away from planning and student support toward repeated checking, correction, and consolidation tasks that should already be systematized.
0102: The registrar workflow shows this problem in concrete terms. Mrs. Jocelyn Cleofe described enrollment and record updates as one-by-one processing with repeated cross-checking across spreadsheets and forms, especially during high-volume periods. According to Grepon et al. (2021), integrated school systems reduce delays and improve record reliability when admissions and records processing operate on one shared data structure. Kanona (2022) further emphasizes that centralized database-backed management improves retrieval speed and record integrity compared with manual, file-fragmented handling.
0103: Finance operations demonstrate the same fragmentation in monetary form. Mrs. Corrine Avellanosa noted that payment updates often require manual cross-referencing before balances are confidently released, particularly when multiple transactions occur close together. According to Wulandari and Pinandito (2021), tuition-related decision support becomes more consistent when financial status logic is structured and system-driven rather than manually interpreted. Chai and Mostafa (2021) similarly report that integrated financial and student records reduce duplication and improve cross-user visibility.
0104: Academic coordination is likewise affected. The registrar and teacher interviews indicate that schedule preparation, class-list alignment, and grade validation involve repetitive verification cycles before release. According to Muller et al. (2025), timetable quality and conflict reduction depend on explicit constraint handling rather than manual plotting. Zulueta et al. (2021) also show that process standardization and workflow discipline are critical in educational MIS environments, reinforcing the need for structured academic operations across sections, subjects, and grade submissions.
0105: Stakeholder communication pressure further amplifies the issue. Parent-facing updates on grades, schedules, and billing are less effective when underlying records are delayed or inconsistently synchronized across offices. According to Navarra and Antonio (2025), web-based monitoring systems improve transparency and usability when authorized users receive timely, role-scoped visibility from one connected record environment. This aligns with Marriott School's need to reduce ad hoc follow-ups and provide dependable status communication to families.
0106: At the leadership level, fragmented records also weaken decision support. Historical data may exist, but when data pipelines are disconnected, trend interpretation becomes late and less actionable. According to Esquivel and Esquivel (2021), enrollment-related decision support improves when institutional records are transformed into forecasting inputs rather than static archives. Hussain et al. (2023) further show that comparative forecasting approaches can provide stronger planning indicators for staffing, sectioning, and resource allocation.
0107: For these reasons, the project context is best understood as an operational integration challenge: multiple offices depend on the same student lifecycle data but require different role-scoped actions. MarriottConnect responds by enforcing one validated data lifecycle from intake, posting, enrichment, and academic processing up to dashboard-ready analytics and governance outputs. In this architecture, data consistency, access control, and decision visibility are treated as interdependent design requirements.
0108: Given this institutional context and evidence base, the following problem statements focus on the remaining strategic question: how an integrated, role-governed, and analytics-capable system can continuously sustain enrollment consistency, finance synchronization, academic reliability, and leadership-ready decision support.
0109: A deeper view of the present environment shows that data fragmentation is not limited to one office transaction. It propagates across the entire student lifecycle, from enrollment intake and payment posting to section assignment, attendance recording, and report generation. According to Zulueta et al. (2021), MIS environments in educational institutions fail when workflow standardization is weak, even when digital tools are available. This finding mirrors Marriott School's operational reality where records may exist in digital format but still require repeated reconciliation before they can be trusted for release, compliance, or planning.
0110: In enrollment periods, the registrar must process a large volume of learner entries under time pressure while preserving LIS-oriented consistency. When intake records are encoded in disconnected trackers, every downstream update introduces a new opportunity for mismatch. According to Porlas et al. (2023), inconsistencies between LIS-facing structures and school-level MIS records create recurring validation burden across offices. For Marriott School, this means that what appears as a small intake encoding delay can later affect payment confirmation, class-list finalization, and administrative reporting confidence.
0111: The record-continuity requirement extends beyond initial enrollment. Permanent records, promotion decisions, and student-departure handling all depend on the same validated learner identity baseline. If this baseline is fragmented, later academic and compliance outputs become harder to verify and slower to release. According to Kanona (2022), structured database-backed systems provide stronger retrieval reliability and control integrity than manual, file-fragmented record handling. This supports MarriottConnect's design principle that learner data should be encoded with continuity in mind rather than re-encoded at every stage of institutional processing.
0112: Finance workflows add another layer of operational sensitivity because billing accuracy directly affects trust, cash-flow transparency, and release-dependent school transactions. The finance interview evidence indicates that balance confirmation often depends on manual sequence checks when multiple payments occur in close succession. According to Wulandari and Pinandito (2021), tuition-related decision support improves when payment-status logic is formalized and system-driven. Chai and Mostafa (2021) also report that integrated student-finance data handling reduces duplicate effort while improving cross-role visibility, which is critical for parent communication and internal reconciliation timing.
0113: Billing transparency is not only an accounting concern; it is a stakeholder-service concern. Families rely on timely balance updates, payment history visibility, and consistent communication channels, especially during high-demand periods. When billing records are delayed, schools face repeated follow-ups that consume office time and weaken confidence in published statements. According to Navarra and Antonio (2025), monitoring platforms improve communication outcomes when data is role-scoped, timely, and accessible through one connected environment. This aligns with MarriottConnect's integrated parent and finance views where institutional records and stakeholder-facing status updates remain synchronized.
0114: Academic scheduling and class-list coordination reveal a similar structural challenge. Subject assignments, section constraints, and teacher availability must be reconciled in ways that prevent conflicts while preserving instructional continuity. Manual cross-checking can complete the task, but it often increases cycle time and revision overhead. According to Muller et al. (2025), timetable quality improves when constraints are explicitly modeled and resolved systematically, not manually improvised. In Marriott School's context, this supports the move toward structured schedule governance where academic planning outputs are reliable before dissemination to teachers, students, and parents.
0115: Instructional workflows also require coherent links between attendance, grading, and verification stages. If class-level records are maintained in disconnected channels, teachers must repeatedly transfer and validate entries before submission, increasing the chance of latency and inconsistency. According to Zulueta et al. (2021), socio-technical alignment in MIS means process rules and system workflows must evolve together to sustain reliability. MarriottConnect addresses this by treating attendance, score encoding, grade submission, and verification checkpoints as connected institutional processes rather than isolated encoding tasks.
0116: Governance and access control requirements further shape the project context. Schools need role boundaries that prevent unauthorized edits while still allowing authorized users to access what they need on time. According to Kanona (2022), central database controls and role-scoped access are key to protecting information integrity in academic systems. The same governance logic is reflected in MarriottConnect through policy-driven access controls, audit visibility, and announcement/notification traceability, so transparency is achieved without sacrificing operational discipline.
0117: Cross-role communication is another decisive factor in institutional performance. When offices and stakeholders rely on ad hoc messaging to confirm record status, coordination overhead grows and official channels lose authority. According to Chai and Mostafa (2021), integrated web-based school information environments improve communication consistency by keeping users connected to one authoritative dataset. This is why MarriottConnect pairs transaction modules with structured announcement and notification workflows: operational decisions and stakeholder communication should reference the same validated institutional data context.
0118: At the leadership tier, the school's need is not only to store records but to interpret operational movement in time for planning. Enrollment growth, payment behavior, grade-submission cadence, and risk indicators all require timely synthesis to support proactive decisions. According to Esquivel and Esquivel (2021), decision-support value increases when institutional records are converted into forecasting-capable signals. Hussain et al. (2023) further demonstrates that trend modeling helps institutions prepare staffing and resource allocations more effectively than static historical review alone.
0119: The implication for Marriott School is clear: reliable decision support depends on operational data continuity, and operational continuity depends on architecture that treats registrar, finance, academic, and stakeholder modules as one coordinated system. According to Grepon et al. (2021), integrated school information architectures improve transaction flow and record reliability by reducing isolated processing channels. Combined with LIS-alignment evidence from Porlas et al. (2023), this reinforces that institutional consistency is produced through design discipline, not post-hoc manual correction.
0120: Therefore, the project context must be interpreted as a systems-level coordination problem with compliance, governance, and stakeholder-service dimensions operating simultaneously. MarriottConnect is positioned to solve this by unifying module workflows, preserving role-based access discipline, and transforming validated operations into analytics-ready evidence for planning. In this framework, the problem statements below are not abstract design questions; they are direct responses to documented process friction and evidence-backed institutional needs observed in Marriott School and reinforced by recent literature.

0122: Statement of the Problem
0123: This study defines the operational problems of Marriott School based on interviews with key school stakeholders in the appendices, particularly the Academic Head (Appendix B.1), Registrar (Appendix B.2), Finance Officer (Appendix B.3), and Teacher (Appendix B.4). Their accounts consistently describe fragmented records, repeated reconciliation, delayed cross-office validation, and limited management visibility during high-volume cycles.
0124: General Problem:
0125: How can MarriottConnect be developed as an integrated student information system with decision support analytics for Marriott School?
0126: According to the Academic Head interview (Appendix B.1), departments still maintain separate files and manually reconcile figures before reports can be finalized. According to the Registrar interview (Appendix B.2), enrollment updates are still processed one-by-one with repeated cross-checking across records. According to the Finance interview (Appendix B.3), payment posting and balance confirmation can be delayed when transactions occur in close succession.
0127: Taken together, these interview findings show that Marriott School's general problem is workflow fragmentation across registrar, finance, and academic operations. Because offices validate the same learner information in different files and at different times, turnaround slows and management receives consolidated information later than needed for timely decisions.
0128: Specific Problems:
0129: How can a centralized registrar workflow be developed to capture enrollment intake, support SF1 upload, and enrich learner records by LRN to reduce duplicate encoding?
0130: According to the Registrar interview (Appendix B.2), intake encoding, profile corrections, and enrichment checks are repeatedly validated across separate files before release. The same interview indicates that this verification burden becomes more severe during enrollment peaks, confirming duplicate encoding and repeated checking as persistent registrar-side bottlenecks.
0131: How can integrated cashiering and billing workflows be developed to automate posting, update balances immediately, and keep ledgers and parent billing views synchronized?
0132: According to the Finance interview (Appendix B.3), payment entries and balance confirmations often require manual cross-referencing before official status can be released. The interview further indicates that when multiple transactions are posted in close succession, reconciliation workload increases, which confirms cashiering and billing synchronization as a real institutional problem.
0133: How can schedule and class-list workflows be developed to support academic planning while minimizing timetable conflicts and repetitive manual verification?
0134: According to the Registrar and Teacher interviews (Appendices B.2 and B.4), schedule checks, class-list preparation, and grade-related validation are repetitive and labor-intensive. Their accounts show that academic coordination slows down when subject, section, and teacher records must be manually verified across disconnected references before release.
0135: How can secure student and parent portals be developed to provide authorized visibility to schedules, grades, and billing information?
0136: According to stakeholder-facing interview evidence in the appendices, delayed record synchronization affects how quickly families receive complete updates. The Finance interview (Appendix B.3) indicates that parent balance communication is not always immediate, and related field accounts show that guardians often rely on follow-ups when records are still being reconciled across offices.
0137: How can role dashboards and governance controls be developed to produce actionable indicators for enrollment trends, payment behavior, and operational risk monitoring?
0138: According to the Academic Head interview (Appendix B.1), leadership reporting is slowed when department outputs come from separate files and require manual consolidation before review. This confirms that management-level indicators for enrollment movement, payment status, and academic monitoring are often delayed by fragmented data preparation practices.

0157: Scope
0158: The proposed system provides module-based access for seven core roles: super admin, admin, registrar, finance, teacher, student, and parent. Access is structured by institutional responsibility and each module groups related actions into one operational unit.
0159: The scope centralizes enrollment, finance, academic, governance, and stakeholder communication workflows while assigning each role to complete modules that include transaction handling, validation, and analytics interpretation.
0160: The Registrar can access the following:
0161: Enrollment Module - Manage intake creation, profile updates, and payment-linked progression within one registrar workflow.
0162: Student Directory Module - Maintain searchable learner profiles, identity continuity, and authorized record retrieval.
0163: SF1 Import Module - Upload SF1 records and execute LRN-based enrichment aligned with LIS-oriented validation rules.
0164: Permanent Records Module - Maintain historical academic records and document continuity across school years.
0165: Batch Promotion and Student Departure Module - Process promotion outcomes and departure workflows under registrar controls.
0166: Registrar Analytics - Review intake movement, enrichment status, record-completion indicators, and registrar risk alerts.
0167: The Finance Officer can access the following:
0168: Cashiering Module - Assess charges, accept payments, and post transactions with immediate balance reflection.
0169: Student Ledger Module - Track debit-credit movement, running balances, and learner-level financial history.
0170: Transaction History Module - Review transaction history and perform authorized void, refund, and reissue actions.
0171: Billing Schedule and Due Allocation Module - Manage due schedules and map posted payments to outstanding obligations.
0172: Fee and Remedial Fee Configuration Module - Maintain standard fee definitions and remedial subject fee policies.
0173: Discount and Scholarship Rules Module - Configure discount policies and apply student-level discount tagging controls.
0174: Inventory Charges Module - Manage finance-related item catalogs and charge integration in cashier workflows.
0175: Financial Analytics - Monitor collection trends, billing risk indicators, reminder outputs, and daily finance performance.
0176: The Teachers can access the following:
0177: Schedule Module - Access assigned teaching loads, class timetables, and advisory schedule context.
0178: Grading and Submission Module  - Configure rubrics, create assessments, encode scores, and submit quarter grades.
0179: Attendance Monitoring Module - Record SF2-aligned attendance statuses linked to subject-assignment context.
0180: Advisory Conduct Module - Encode conduct indicators and advisory observations for class-level monitoring.
0181: Gradebook Validation Module - Review grading completeness, submission status, and correction checkpoints.
0182: Instructional Analytics  - Analyze class performance trends, completion alerts, and quarter progress indicators.
0183: The Parents can access the following:
0184: Schedule Module - View child schedules, class timing, and section-related timetable context.
0185: Billing Module - Track dues schedules, payment history, and current balance snapshots.

0187: Academic Records Module - Access child’s quarter and final grade records under role-scoped permissions.

0205: Limitations
0206: While MarriottConnect provides integrated coverage of registrar, finance, academic, and stakeholder workflows, the study remains bounded by institutional context, operational policy conditions, and data quality dependencies that influence measured outcomes.
0207: For this study, the following limitations apply:
0208: System evaluation is grounded in one host institution (Marriott School); cross-school generalization requires additional deployments and comparative validation in other school environments.
0209: LIS-aligned processing depends on sustained institutional data discipline, including accurate intake encoding, timely SF1 uploads, and consistent LRN validation across operational cycles.
0210: Analytics outputs are based on available institutional historical records; forecasting and trend interpretation quality remains dependent on the completeness and consistency of encoded transactions.
0211: Operational performance remains dependent on reliable internet connectivity, available desktop access for guarded workflows, and sustained user adherence to defined role procedures.
0212: Policy, curriculum, and regulatory updates may require periodic configuration adjustments to preserve alignment between institutional operations and report-generation standards.
0213: These limitations define the study context and evaluation boundaries while preserving the system's value as an integrated operational and decision-support platform.

0219: Local Studies 
0220: Designing and Implementing e-School Systems: An Information Systems Approach to School Management of a Community College in Northern Mindanao, Philippines
0221: Grepon et al. (2021) conducted an applied research investigation into the creation of an integrated admission, record processing, and reporting system based on a unified digital work-flow. They qualified this research by demonstrating that if a school would work from a single set of shared data (rather than multiple sets of disconnected files) the time to process records and the reliability of records would improve. The study emphasized practical implementation behavior, including workflow continuity across offices and usability outcomes for non-technical institutional users.
0222: This source is highly relevant to MarriottConnect because it directly supports the system's central design objective: convert fragmented office transactions into a single source of truth. In the appendices, the Registrar repeatedly described one-by-one updates and manual double-checking as a major bottleneck; Grepon et al. (2021) provides empirical support for replacing that process with integrated registrar-to-finance-to-academic data continuity. The study also justifies the system's modular but connected architecture, where each office retains role boundaries while operating on shared records.
0223: A Comparative Case Study on the Challenges Encountered by Philippine Private and Public Educational Institutions with their Existing Management Information System
0224: In their study, Zulueta and colleagues (2021) analyzed challenges related to the implementation of Management Information Systems (MIS) that had been experienced in the Philippine Education System. The authors determined that there were ongoing issues relating to the standardization of processes, readiness of physical infrastructure, user adaptation, and operational support mechanisms based on real-world experiences. In addition to considering technical characteristics of MIS applications as a parameter for measuring performance, the authors also evaluated the effectiveness of these systems through a socio-technical lens (interaction of the organizational work structure and the quality of MIS system design) and emphasized that coordination of these two elements was vital for effective implementation.
0225: For MarriottConnect, this study is relevant because the appendices show that current delays are not caused by a single office failure but by cross-office coordination friction. Zulueta et al. (2021) supports the capstone's staged rollout logic and reinforces the need for clear role-specific workflows, consistent operational rules, and adoption-oriented implementation practices. It also strengthens the rationale for including governance-related controls and maintainability planning, not just transactional modules.
0226: A Machine Learning Based DSS in Predicting Undergraduate Freshmen Enrolment in a Philippine University
0227: Esquivel and Esquivel (2021) demonstrated how machine-learning-based decision support can transform historical enrollment records into forecasting outputs usable for institutional planning. The study showed that data-driven prediction methods can reduce reliance on manual trend estimation and improve preparation for upcoming academic periods. Its contribution is particularly valuable because it connects routine data storage with forward-looking administrative action.
0228: This is directly applicable to MarriottConnect's analytics direction, where enrollment trend interpretation is expected to support section planning, staffing preparation, and capacity decisions. In the appendices, school leadership noted difficulty producing consolidated trend insights from dispersed records; Esquivel and Esquivel (2021) provides a methodological bridge from transactional history to planning intelligence. The study therefore supports the inclusion of forecasting-ready analytics in the administrative dashboard model.
0229: Comparative Analysis on the Efficiency of LIS Vis-a-Vis SMIS: A Case Study
0230: Porlas et al. (2023) compared the efficiency of DepEd LIS processes and school-level MIS workflows, emphasizing the operational consequences of inconsistent synchronization between compliance-oriented and institution-level records. The study highlighted that when local school systems do not align cleanly with reporting structures, institutions face additional validation burden and inconsistent data interpretation across offices.
0231: This finding is highly relevant to MarriottConnect because the system is intentionally designed for LIS-aware processing rather than isolated internal encoding. The staged model used in the capstone intake, cashier posting, and SF1/LRN-based enrichment directly addresses the synchronization challenge discussed by Porlas et al. (2023). The source strengthens the justification for treating enrollment data quality and reporting alignment as core architectural requirements, not post-processing tasks.
0232: Efficient Student Monitoring and Data Tracking System
0233: The research of Navarra & Antonio (2025) showed that by using a web-based approach for tracking and monitoring students, teachers, students and parents obtained positive results in terms of both functionality and usability. The findings of the study demonstrate the importance of having timely access to information about the school, having clear lines of communication between institutions and parents, and being able to access timely information about their child’s academic progress.
0234: For MarriottConnect, this is strongly relevant to stakeholder-facing modules. Appendix responses indicate that parents often experience delayed or incomplete updates, while offices rely on fragmented communication channels to resolve record issues. Navarra and Antonio (2025) supports the system's parent and student portal strategy, where authorized users can access updated academic and billing-related information through one secure platform rather than informal or delayed communication paths.
0235: International Studies
0236: Designing Decision Support System for Midwifery Students' Tuition Fees Problem
0237: Wulandari and Pinandito (2021) proposed a decision support model for tuition delinquency management using structured decision logic. Their study moved beyond simple balance viewing by showing how financial status classification can be translated into actionable policy options for school management. The research demonstrated that decision-support logic can improve consistency and speed in handling financially sensitive student cases.
0238: This aligns directly with MarriottConnect's finance goals, particularly delinquency identification and actionable payment-status interpretation. 0412: Design of Software, System, Product, and/or Processes
0413: The software design of MarriottConnect follows a role-centered, modular architecture that separates registrar, finance, academic, stakeholder, and governance concerns while maintaining one shared institutional data model.
0414: At the backend layer, Laravel route groups and middleware enforce role boundaries so each user enters only authorized modules and actions. This design prevents cross-role data misuse while preserving workflow continuity across connected processes.
0415: At the frontend layer, Inertia.js with React and TypeScript provides component-based pages that map directly to role workflows. Shared UI patterns are used to keep task flow predictable for high-frequency operations such as intake, posting, grading, and verification.
0416: The data design is relational and continuity-oriented: learner identity, enrollment state, financial transactions, schedules, attendance, and grades are linked through validated references so updates are reusable across modules without repeated encoding.
0417: Process design is built around staged operational flow. Registrar intake initializes learner context, finance posting updates balance state, enrichment validates identity continuity, and academic modules consume authorized records for schedule and grading outputs.
0418: Analytics design uses standardized KPI, alert, and trend payload contracts so dashboards across roles can render consistent indicators while still reflecting role-specific context. This allows school leadership and operational staff to interpret metrics from one coherent data pipeline.
0419: Governance design integrates authentication, permission visibility, audit traceability, and configurable system controls. Together, these controls support accountability, policy alignment, and secure institutional operation.
0420: Overall, the product design treats MarriottConnect not as isolated feature pages but as one coordinated school operations platform where transaction quality and decision visibility are engineered as connected outcomes.
